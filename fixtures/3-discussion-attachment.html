Arts Discussion Forum #9: The Role of Vulnerability, Risk, and Authenticity in Performance

This week, we explore live performance as a space of connection, risk, and shared experience. Unlike edited recordings, live performances happen in real time—with no retakes or filters. Their power comes not from perfection, but from the raw, authentic moments exchanged between performer and audience.

Think about the last time you witnessed a live performance—whether music, theatre, or dance. There were no retakes, no safety nets. Every note, every movement, every moment was alive with risk. In an age where we can stream polished performances from the comfort of our homes, why does live performance—unfiltered and unpredictable—still resonate so deeply with us as audience members?

Step 1: Choose one or more resources to engage with

Each link below explores vulnerability in various performance art forms:

  • Music: Why Musicians Should Embrace Their Vulnerability explores how emotional openness isn’t just brave—it’s an invitation to genuine connection.

  • Theatre: Embracing Vulnerability invites us to consider what it takes to bring one’s full, imperfect self to the stage.

  • Dance: How to Be Vulnerable Onstage Without Losing Control reflects on how embracing vulnerability can deepen physical expression and unlock authenticity..

  • The Quest for Craft: Season 3 | Chapter 10: Yo-Yo Ma, where the renowned cellist explores how the pursuit of artistic excellence demands embracing vulnerability and the willingness to share one’s craft openly in the moment of performance.

Step 2: Reflect on a live performance

Choose one of the following reflection paths:

  • If you’ve performed live:
    Think of a specific moment. What risks were you taking How did unpredictability (from the audience, the space, or your emotions) affect your performance Did it enhance your connection—or feel overwhelming?

  • If you haven’t performed:
    Think of a live performance you’ve attended that moved you. What made it memorable How did the performer’s presence or vulnerability impact your experience?

Step 3: Make connections

Refer to something from this week’s readings or video that resonated with you. How did it deepen your understanding of what it takes to be vulnerable onstage?

Step 4: Consider the role of ritual

Performances often feel like rituals—structured yet transformative moments that create shared emotional experiences. How might this ritual-like quality impact the way performers prepare and audiences connect with the performance In what ways could this structure help relieve stress, or contribute to it?

Step 5: Respond to a classmate

After posting, reply to at least one peer. Share a similar or contrasting experience, offer a new perspective, or ask a question that furthers the conversation.

  • 300 word minimum; include word count at the end of your submission
  • Include at least one thoughtful reply (minimum 25 words) to a classmate’s post in the forum.
  • All Discussion Forum posts must be your own work. Do not use AI tools, grammar checkers, or any other assistance to generate or edit your responses.

Reply from Sierra Richardson

I've danced many times often the same dances, but my group toured to different places and we usually had no idea who our audience was going to be or what we'd be dancing on. Sometimes it was cobblestone road other times we were dancing on a little dock by a lake. This unpredictability left us wondering if we would slip or stumble or even just make it practically impossible for us to dance in our typical dance shoes. I felt so overwhelmed because there's always an expectation to perform well with no mistakes. It's something that get's drilled into you if you've done it long enough.

However, on these occasions where we felt the risk of dancing our director reminded us that the audience just wants to feel something. Make them laugh, bring them joy and let the mistakes be a part of the routine. Of course we always did our darndest, but I love how Peter's talks about how by being vulnerable we allow the audience to relate to us on a deeper level. I've never viewed it that way, but looking back I couldn't agree more. Although we slipped at times or we made mistakes the people loved it when we got back up and that alone represented our theme and overall message. Without this vulnerability we became unrelatable or something that could be replaced by a video recording. These unexpected moments is what brings dancing it's joys and challenges us to relate in ways we might not have gotten to.

Technical excellence alone is not enough to bring the performance life. In this way it is very much like a ritual, by the end of a ritual you'd expect some transformation. This transformation doesn't happen by just walking through the steps, it happens when you allow yourself to be vulnerable and emotionally present on stage.

Reply from Matt Withers

The first time I performed a solo I was genuinely nervous in a way that affected the actual sound coming out of my mouth. My voice constricted and tightened up to the point where I could hear it changing while I was singing, which is an uncomfortable thing to experience in real time in front of people. There was no way to hide it or go back and try again. That vulnerability is challenging and it made the performance feel like something I was surviving rather than sharing.
By the time I performed my last solo things were different. Months of lessons and dedicated practice on a single song meant that when I stood up to sing I had something to fall back on. The preparation did not eliminate the nerves but it gave me enough confidence that the vulnerability shifted from something overwhelming to something that actually connected me to the music. The article on musicians and vulnerability made a point that resonated with me — that emotional openness is not just about being brave but about genuinely inviting the audience into the experience. That is something I felt more in my last performance than my first.
Choir was its own version of this. After almost two years of regular performances it started to feel almost routine, which I think is the risk that comes with repetition. When I was not paying close attention it could feel mechanical, but when I actually tuned in and listened to what was happening around me it was still a genuinely moving experience. Copland's idea of active listening applies here too — the music does not change but your engagement with it does.
The ritual aspect of performance is something I had not thought about much before this module but it makes a lot of sense. The rehearsals, the warmup, walking out on stage — all of it creates a structure that can either calm you down or wind you up depending on your relationship with it. For me the ritual of preparation was mostly grounding, but the moment right before performing still carried its own specific kind of weight that no amount of practice fully removes.

Reply from Allie Moore

I have performed live multiple times, in choir, speech/debate, and theatre when I was in high school. One specific time that I am reminded of when I was reading the articles about vulnerability, especially in music and theatre, was when I performed a piece from Girl Interrupted for a speech and debate tournament. The piece was highly visceral and I had to let go of the performance of 'normal' that we all do to fit into society and instead tap into my raw emotions and vulnerability to convey the story I was portraying. This I think contrasts what a lot of us think of as performance, I think a lot of us have the idea that performance is about putting on something 'else' something 'other' but from my experience we are more constantly not ourselves in our regular lives in an effort for survival and acceptance and performance is where we get to let our real emotional, broken, strong, powerful selves shine because as long as the audience thinks it a performance, they find your intensity, ridiculousness, anger, sadness, etc. acceptable. I feel the same way when I dance which I have never done performatively but I still love the feeling of just doing whatever the heck feels right with my body and the music and that is more the real me than the person quietly walking through a store as my favorite song plays. So I argue that the reason you should be vulnerable as an artist is that life is the real performance and art is where reality actually shows its face. 

This ritual of the artist dropping the mask they wear in everyday life and letting their emotions shine through and connect with the audience is why live performances will always exist. You simply cannot create the same emotional connection when you are not in the room with the person. Recorded performances can create an emotional response but not the same emotional connection. There is also something to be said about experiencing the raw emotions of a performer as they are portraying them. These shared rituals are part of what make performance and art so integral to the human experience.

Reply from Haley Banks

I decided to focus on the dance link to engage with. In high school, I helped create a dance for my high school dance. This allowed me to have a first-hand understanding of risk, vulnerability, and authenticity that goes into a live performance. Unlike a recorded video, where the mistakes can be taken out, there was only one chance to make sure the dance was perfect in front of a lot of people. There was a lot of pressure riding on me because anything could happen rather, I was prepared or not. Even though there were tons of practice before the performance, there is still uncertainty when it comes down to the real thing. 

A big risk was putting myself out there in front of those people. There was a ton of hours put into learning and rehearsing the dance. Then when it came to performing, it opened us up to others judging us on something we worked so hard to make possible. On the other hand, the vulnerability of performing allowed us to connect with the viewers on a different level. When the performance began my stomach was in my feet, but I also felt so excited. The energy from the crowd allowed me to be calm, not overwhelmed, and the audience reaction helped increase my confidence. 

This week's material allowed me to learn why those feeling I experienced are important in the performing world. In the Yo-Yo Ma video, he discussed the importance of being open and sharing one's talents with others around us. The experience taught me that a performance does not have to be big or even perfect to have an impact on others. Overall, my prom dance showed me how powerful liver performance is. The power of it combines preparation, risk, though fullness, and authenticity of all the performers. The possibility of mistakes makes a person stronger and the experience real. It also allows for a stronger connection between all the viewers and the performers.

Reply from Cannon Downs

I haven't had many live performances, but back in middle school, I had a band class. Part of this, we had a few performances over the year. Our concerts were a weird experience, partly because I had never performed before, but also because no one else had. There was a nervousness that I knew most people were feeling. All of us were very far from perfect. I knew that for me, I had a hard time not knowing what was going to happen. We had practiced and rehearsed, but it was the thought of messing up that was hard for me. If someone messed up, it would be pretty clear. In my mind, that simply meant, messing up is bad. For me, that overwhelmed me a little bit. Instead of making me want to be better and continue to pursue being good, it made me want to shy away.

In the article, Why Musicians Should Embrace Vulnerability,  it talks about this same thing. A lot of musicians, at much higher level than a middle school band, feel that pressure when put in a vulnerable position. But it is good to feel that pressure. It helps to encourage growth. Live performance don't have safety nets and that helps to grow the performers. It encourages not perfection, but a different frame of mind. The frame of mind helps to push performances, and make each live performance unique and genuine.

Performances can have a ritual like vibe, at least for performers. They may prepare the same way each time, or have things they do for luck's sake. This may help performers to get in the right frame of mind. I don't think that this contributes to stress, but it helps performers to deal with it in their own way. Each person needs their own ways to deal with the pressure that will help shape and improve themselves.
