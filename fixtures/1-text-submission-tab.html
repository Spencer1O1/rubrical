Module 4: Cultural Event Review #2

Module 4: Cultural Event Review #2
Due: Fri Jul 10, 2026 11:59pmDue: Fri Jul 10, 2026 11:59pm7/10/2026
Ungraded, 30 Possible Points30 Points Possible
In Progress
NEXT UP: Submit Assignment
Unlimited Attempts Allowed

A significant portion of the course points will come from attending and reflecting on three types of cultural events—one each in Visual Art, Music, and Theatre/Dance that you experience this semester. Reviews may be completed in any order. Whenever possible, you are encouraged to attend live events: not only do they require only one reflection instead of multiple online ones, they are often far more enjoyable and inspiring, allowing for a deeper connection with the work, the artists, and the atmosphere. Plan your events early to allow for thoughtful selection and meaningful engagement. 

Follow this format for your review (with separate paragraphs for each section). If submitting multiple reports for events with moderate (2) or low (1) engagement, please list them consecutively below.

  • Event Details (3 points):
    Include the event name, location, date, and start time, as well as some kind of documentation of your participation (a selfie, a scan of your ticket, a screenshot of the event’s online ad, etc.).
  • Event Category & Engagement Point Level (3 points):
    Identify the event category (music, visual art, theater/dance) and rate its engagement level (high=3, moderate=2, low=1) based on the criteria below. The engagement point total should be at least 3 for each Cultural Event Review submission.
  • General Overview (8 points):
    (100-200 words): Describe the event’s atmosphere, setting, and presentation. Try to specifically describe any formal aspects of the art you observed to the best of your ability, using our textbook and other online resources as aids.
  • Personal Experience & Reflection (8 points):
    (100-200 words): Share what stood out or surprised you. Reflect on how it connected (or how it didn’t connect) with you personally and what the overall experience revealed about your relationship with this type of art.
  • Art, Society & Audience Engagement 8 points):
    (100-200 words): Explain how the audience (if present) interacted with the event. If the experience was online, asynchronous, or lacked a clear live audience (like a podcast or public art piece), reflect on how the work imagines or reaches its audience. How effective was this performance in engaging the audience, in your opinion?

Each Cultural Event Review submission must total 3 category points. You may combine live and online events to reach the 3-point minimum.  In other words, you would only need to submit 1 review for a live event, but 3 reviews for all online content. If you have any questions about a particular event’s category, reach out for guidance from your assigned UTF.

Category (3 total points required)

3 points (High Engagement, In-Person)

2 points (Moderate Engagement, In-Person or Hybrid)

1 point (Low Engagement, Online)

Visual Art
  • Museum visit (this refers specifically to an art museum, where the primary focus is on visual or artistic works; visits to history, science, or other museums without a clear emphasis on art do not fulfill this requirement)
  • Professional or university gallery exhibition
  • Art walk or city art crawl
  • Art festival with juried exhibitors
  • Architecture tour or design-focused exhibition (e.g., historic buildings, contemporary design spaces)
  • In-person artist talk or Q&A session
  • Cultural or heritage arts fair
  • Farmer’s market with art sellers
  • Art boutique or commercial art storefront
  • Informal pop-up gallery or immersive installation (with explanation of context included)
  • Online gallery or museum tour (include link)
  • Curated Instagram/TikTok gallery by a known artist (with background context included; include link)
  • Online art workshop or demo (include link)
  • Podcast episode about a visual art topic (include link)

Music
  • Live concert at a professional or university level (orchestra, jazz band, opera, touring band, etc.)
  • Music festival or other ticketed performance (e.g., classical, folk, jazz, rock)
  • Intimate performance setting (e.g., house concert, pop-up show, or small-venue acoustic set encouraging close audience-performer connection) 
  • In-person music lecture or Q&A session
  • Music in a relaxed background format (coffeehouse, restaurant, open mic night, wedding, etc.)
  • Local community, church, or pre-college school performance
  • Informal jam session or rehearsal (e.g., garage band, community group, drum circle, peer ensemble meetup)
  • Online concert (40+ minutes, include YouTube or streaming link)
  • Recorded Tiny Desk Concerts or NPR Live sessions (include link)
  • Digital album release events with livestream Q&A (include link)
  • Podcast episode about a music topic (include link)
  • Virtual masterclass or webinar with a musician or ensemble (include link)

Theatre/Dance
  • Live performance at a professional or university level (any genre—play, musical theatre, modern dance, etc.)
  • Staged reading or workshop production with artist interaction
  • Site-specific or immersive performance with active audience participation (e.g., improv theatre, interactive dance, environmental or promenade theatre)
  • In-person theatre or dance lecture, Q&A with a playwright or choreographer, or dramaturgy session
  • Pre-college production (elementary, middle, or high school; children’s theatre) or other amateur performance
  • Observation of a theatre or dance workshop or class (e.g., acting, stage combat, playwriting, ballet)
  • Social dance event (e.g., ballroom, nightclub, swing, or country line dancing)
  • Online stage production (40+ minutes, include YouTube link)
  • Recorded play or musical on a streaming service (include link)
  • Play reading via Zoom or recorded table read (include link)
  • Dance performance film or documentary (40+ minutes, include link)
  • Podcast episode about a theatre or dance topic (include link)

Note on Film and Cinema
While cinema is a powerful and meaningful artistic medium, attending movies (including mainstream, independent, or international films) does not fulfill the requirements for a Cultural Event Review.
